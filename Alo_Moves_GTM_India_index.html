The Real India Context

What makes this market genuinely different

🇮🇳 India-Specific Market Dynamics

The competitor isn't Cult.fit. It's free YouTube yoga.

The real competition
Channels like Yoga With Adriene, Cult.fit's free content, and thousands of Hindi-language YouTube creators offer unlimited free yoga — and Indians are already using them.
The price psychology problem
₹999/mo feels expensive when quality free alternatives exist. This is not a pricing problem — it's a value communication problem. The pitch cannot be "great yoga content."
Tier 2 city opportunity
Delhi and Mumbai are the obvious entry points, but Tier 2 cities (Pune, Hyderabad, Bengaluru) have high smartphone penetration and lower competition from offline studios — potentially better unit economics.
Language nuance
English-only UX limits reach even in urban markets. Hindi subtitles or a Hindi instructor track for select programs would expand the addressable audience significantly.
My Reasoning

Why I reframed the competitive set

Most GTM strategies for India pitch against other paid apps. That's the wrong comparison set. If the user is choosing between Alo Moves and Cult.fit, that's a product comparison. But if they're choosing between Alo Moves and a free YouTube playlist they already love — that's a fundamentally different conversion challenge. The strategy has to answer: "Why pay when I can get yoga for free?" The answer is consistency, not content. That reframe changes everything from pricing to messaging to onboarding.

My Reasoning

Why "guilty about paying" is the most important pain point

Priya's biggest barrier isn't price — it's justifying the price to herself when free alternatives exist. The marketing messaging must preemptively answer this: "You've tried free. You know it doesn't stick." The conversion story is about the cost of inconsistency, not the cost of the subscription. Once that reframe lands, ₹999/month feels reasonable.

Positioning Strategy

Not more content. A system for consistency.

Most fitness apps in India compete on content volume. Free YouTube channels have already won that battle. Alo Moves' differentiator is its structured, program-based design — built for habit formation, not casual browsing.

Positioning Statement: For urban Indian millennials and Gen Z who value mindfulness but struggle with consistency, Alo Moves offers structured, expert-led yoga programs designed to build lasting habits. Unlike free content (which is infinite but formless), Alo Moves gives you a system — week-by-week progression, accountability, and a clear sense of progress.

Structured

System

Stress-first

My Reasoning

Why the annual plan is the most important lever in India

At ₹999/month, every monthly renewal is a fresh decision — "should I keep paying?" The annual plan eliminates that decision. At ₹500/month, the comparison to free content becomes much easier to win. I'd push the annual plan aggressively at the end of the 7-day trial, when the user has experienced value but hasn't yet formed the "cancel" habit.

My Reasoning

Why 20% trial-to-paid is the hardest number and what I'd do about it

20% is aggressive for a brand-new market entrant, especially against a free-content baseline. I'd expect 10–15% more realistically in Month 1. To move toward 20%, the activation experience in the first 3 sessions is everything — users need to feel visible, measurable progress before the trial ends. I'd invest in a strong day-1 onboarding flow: "Tell us your goal → Here's your 7-day program → Session 1 starts now." Reducing time-to-first-value is the highest-leverage optimisation in the funnel.

Target: "yoga app India", "meditation for stress", "morning yoga routine". Retarget website visitors with trial CTA emphasising the free 7-day window.

Blog targeting "workplace stress India", "yoga for beginners", "morning routine for professionals". Long-term organic acquisition engine — compound ROI over 6–12 months.
Priority: Test
Corporate Wellness Partnerships
Underused channel. Partner with Bengaluru/Delhi tech companies for subsidised employee access. B2B2C route into the exact target persona at scale with no paid CAC.
My Reasoning

Why I added corporate wellness as a channel — and why it's underrated

The target user (Priya, stressed urban professional) is sitting in an office right now. Corporate wellness programs are a direct channel to her at zero paid CAC — the company subsidises or recommends the app. In India, tech companies especially in Bengaluru, Hyderabad, and Gurugram actively look for mental wellness benefits post-pandemic. A B2B2C channel could deliver 10× better CAC than paid social if executed early.

Execution Plan

Phased GTM timeline

Phase 1 — Months 1–2
Pre-Launch: Build credibility before asking for money
KPIs: Waitlist signups, content engagement, organic reach
  • Publish YouTube series: "30-day stress reset" — free, full sessions to build trust
  • SEO blogs targeting "mental wellness for working professionals India"
  • Instagram Reels: program preview content + instructor spotlights
  • Waitlist with early-access incentive: extended 14-day trial (vs standard 7)
  • Initiate conversations with 3–5 Bengaluru tech companies for corporate pilots
Phase 2 — Months 2–3
Launch: Drive installs and trial activations
KPIs: App installs, trial signups, Day-7 activation rate, session completion
  • Launch Google + YouTube paid campaigns (Hindi + English ad copy)
  • Partner with 5–8 mid-tier wellness influencers for launch push
  • PR outreach: Femina, Vogue India, YourStory, Healthshots
  • ASO optimisation with Hindi keyword set live on Day 1
  • Activate waitlist with personal launch email — urgency + exclusivity framing
Phase 3 — Months 3–5
Post-Launch: Optimise the trial-to-paid conversion
KPIs: Trial-to-paid CVR, 30-day retention, CAC by channel, annual plan uptake
  • Scale best-performing ad creatives, pause underperformers ruthlessly
  • Push notifications and streak mechanics to reduce trial churn
  • Annual plan upsell: triggered email on Day 5 of trial (before expiry anxiety kicks in)
  • Gather user feedback → improve onboarding flow → test new D1 experience
  • Launch referral: "Gift a week of Alo Moves" — shareable, low-friction
  • Evaluate corporate wellness pilot results and decide B2B2C investment level
Strategic Thesis

The core hypothesis

What I believe about this market

Consistency is the product. Free content already lost the battle for Priya's attention — Alo Moves has to win the battle for her habit.

The Indian wellness market is saturated with free, high-quality content. The user's problem is not access — it's follow-through. Every retention mechanic (streaks, guided programs, daily reminders, week-by-week progression) is a solution to the consistency problem. The brand's job is to make that frame land clearly before the first session, not after the trial ends. If we nail the first 3 days, we win. If we don't, no amount of paid acquisition will fix the churn.

What I'd Do Differently If I Were Running This

I'd launch in Bengaluru first, not Delhi or Mumbai

Bengaluru has the highest concentration of English-speaking, salaried tech professionals — Alo Moves' exact persona — with more disposable income and lower competition from offline yoga studios than Delhi. It's also the corporate wellness hub. A focused Bengaluru-first launch lets you validate the GTM model before scaling spend to more expensive markets. City-level launches also generate local word-of-mouth that pan-India campaigns don't.
